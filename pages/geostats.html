Geography Statistical Skills

A comprehensive guide to the core statistical tests and measures required for A Level Geography and NEA coursework.

Measures of Central Tendency

Mean

When to use: To find the mathematical average of a continuous dataset (e.g., average river depth, mean annual temperature, average pebble size).

Positives

  • Uses every single value in the dataset, making it mathematically precise.
  • It is required as a baseline calculation for higher-level statistics, like Standard Deviation.

Negatives

  • Highly skewed and distorted by extreme anomalies (e.g., one massive pebble makes the whole sample seem larger).
  • Can result in impossible geographical decimals (e.g., 2.4 pedestrians).

Median

When to use: To find the exact middle value of an ordered dataset. Highly useful when your geographical data is heavily skewed (e.g., income levels in a ward).

Positives

  • Completely unaffected by extreme outliers or anomalies in the data.
  • Gives a much more realistic "typical" value if the data is heavily skewed to one side.

Negatives

  • Ignores the actual values of most data points (only cares about their rank/order).
  • Can be slow and tedious to calculate manually for very large datasets because data must be ordered first.

Mode

When to use: To identify the most frequently occurring value or category. Perfect for nominal (categoric) data, like the most common rock type on a beach or predominant land use.

Positives

  • The only measure of central tendency that can be used for categoric (worded) data.
  • Completely unaffected by extreme anomalies.

Negatives

  • Does not use all of the data.
  • There might not be a mode (if all values are unique), or there could be multiple modes (bimodal), making it confusing to interpret.
Measures of Dispersion

Range

When to use: To find the total spread of your dataset from the lowest recorded value to the highest.

Positives

  • Incredibly quick and easy to calculate.
  • Gives an immediate, simple understanding of the total width/spread of the geographical data.

Negatives

  • Extremely sensitive to anomalies (it only uses the two most extreme values in the entire dataset).
  • Tells you absolutely nothing about how the data is distributed between the highest and lowest values.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

When to use: To measure the spread of the middle 50% of your data. Often visualized using box plots (e.g., comparing the core spread of environmental quality scores across two streets).

Positives

  • Eliminates the impact of extreme upper and lower outliers.
  • Excellent for comparing the main "bulk" of two different datasets to see where the majority of data lies.

Negatives

  • Ignores exactly half of your data (the top 25% and bottom 25%).
  • More complex and time-consuming to calculate manually compared to the simple range.

Standard Deviation

When to use: To measure the average distance of all data points from the mean. A small SD means data is tightly clustered (reliable mean), a large SD means data is spread out.

Positives

  • Uses every single piece of data in the set, making it highly robust.
  • Excellent for proving the reliability of the mean.
  • Allows for normal distribution analysis (e.g., 68% of data falls within 1 standard deviation).

Negatives

  • Requires a complex, multi-step calculation that is very easy to mess up manually.
  • Because it squares the differences from the mean, extreme outliers can still heavily distort the final result.
